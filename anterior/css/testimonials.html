I value my time spent in Ecuador so much it's hard to find the words to express my thanks. What I saw, did, and generally experienced there are things I will always cherish. I suppose I'll start by telling you a bit about what we did there. Our group got a full dose of Ecuador, skipping no part of the potion. David gave us time in Quito, the orient/jungle, the coast and all that lies in-between. Ecuador is so diverse that it often felt as if we were stepping into a different country as we traveled to a different place. Most predominantly, the people in Ecuador, though their lifestyle and surrounding were so various, all held the same spirit in them. Though they often had so little in possessions, they were more giving than anyone I know and the good atmosphere they created was palpable in their presence. My family in Las Mercedes, the town in which we did our homestays, was so welcoming I immediately felt like family; 2 seconds into their home. At Yachana High School too, where in the US a school of 100 boys tensions would run high, the students were only concerned with having a good time.
Now, while the people of Ecuador are lovely, the place they live in is gorgeous. The jungle seethes with life and the beaches are as beautiful, if not more so, than any we have here. Sitting at the foot of the Andes' mountains one is overwhelmed by their power. So huge and still, yet so moving. I did more traveling by bus during this trip than I have ever done before so I got to see large swaths of the land that way as well. The rolling hills could seem endless at times, with the checkered pattern of small farms on them making it seem as if we were going in circles. We got accustomed to seeing livestock everywhere, though a chicken riding in one of our buses still managed to surprise the group with a squawk. And of course milking a cow was an enlightening experience for all of us.
I think my favorite part of our travels was the too few nights we spent on the beach. The hostels we stayed in were nice but I couldn't wait to get out of them and out into the surroundings. The energy that life on the coast holds is immediately apparent upon stepping off the bus. From the fish markets to the voracious tourist agents, everything moves with a purpose and appetite. Even the whales, of which there are amazing numbers, seem to be charged with energy. We rode next to one as it breached upwards of 20 times! Naturally I was partial to the ocean itself, which me and Erin (fellow Encuentros member) went on a couple of lengthy swims in. The warm water was irresistible and a shocking blue. I felt like I could stay in forever. The beaches too were awesome and much more alive than the ones we have here in California. There were swarms of crabs running around, live sand dollars to be found in the surf, and cool pieces of coral EVERYWHERE. We even got to go snorkeling off La Isla de Plata and saw all sorts of varieties of fish, coral, and even a ray. The theme of seeing something you'd only catch in a zoo back at home was carried throughout our travels. I could go on for hours about the fun things there were to see. I have, for instance, been completely desensitized to giant scary spiders. The one's they have there are built like tanks, have big spikes, or can kill quickly with a single bite. I also got to swing on an actual jungle vine! It felt like being Tarzan. The buses are terrifying but it was quite a laugh to see a couple of Jackie Chan movies on one of our rides. Classic globalization.
Now that I'm home I can sit back and see what I learned from this trip. For one, it's nice to have concrete memories of the experiences. These are in comparison to the trips my family made when we were little which just feel like a haze now. I feel that at this age, I can fully appreciate and learn from my experiences. In addition, the trip left me with a feeling of comfort with the world around me. Somehow, trekking around a country with sketchy infrastructure where you can never be exactly sure of what to expect has exorcised me of the fear I would occasionally feel when encountering something new in my day to day life. This has given me a welcome new feeling of independence in and out of my home. Furthermore, I'm not entirely sure what it was but after this summer I have felt much less self-conscious. I no longer give a darn if others think my clothes are funny or who I hang out with is cool. I focus much more now on understanding the why and how of my life and try to be less caught up in the superficial that can so easily take over.
This is all due to you, Tad. I can't thank you enough. Without your help my summer would have been fun, but nothing compared to the unforgettable experience Ecuador pulled out of its pocket. After all, how can lying on a beach compare to riding a zipline 300 feet over a river?
Peter, Encuentros 2009, California, age 16
